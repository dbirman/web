Mechanisms of Attention

with Justin Gardner



Does Attention to Visual Features Interact in Predictable Ways?

You might be surprised to learn about how often visual awareness fails. Remarkably, you can be attending to a specific feature in an image (say a climber, high on a rock wall) and entirely unaware of other aspects of the image. The surprise is that you don't know that you weren't aware of the rest of the image, in fact you feel like you know exactly what was there.

This effect is called inattentional blindness (Mack & Rock 1998) and is easily reproduced in the laboratory. It appears that no stimulus can evade inattentional blindness. Even gist perception, which was long thought to be immune (Li et al. 2002) is actually diminished by attention to a difficult motion-tracking task (Cohen et al. 2011). But how does attention cause these effects? Are some inattentional blindness effects stronger than others? If yes, why?

This project has three moving parts, each of which is designed to help us understand one aspect of these important questions:

  1. We are investigating whether hierarchical position in the visual stream is important for how visual features interact.
  2. We are looking at whether attentional effects can be measured and connected to behavior via neurobiologicaly plausible models.
  3. Finally, we are looking at whether these effects manifest in architecturally distinct but computationally similar convolutional neural networks.


This project is a collaboration with Justin Gardner, Professor in the Dept. of Psychology at Stanford University. Gardner Lab; Code; Stim
